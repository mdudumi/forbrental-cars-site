Explore Albania: Cars, Driving & Culture

Your go-to source for detailed guides on renting cars, navigating Albanian roads safely, and uncovering the rich history and unique curiosities of Albania—from Tirana’s bustling streets to the ancient ruins of Butrint.

Renting Cars in Albania

Whether you’re touching down at Tirana International Airport or picking up in the city center, understanding local rental practices ensures a smooth start to your journey.

Most agencies require a valid driver’s license (issued at least one year prior), passport or ID card, and a credit card for the security deposit. Optional insurance packages range from basic third-party coverage to comprehensive collision damage waivers—choose based on your comfort level and itinerary.

  • Book early during summer (June–August) to secure best rates.
  • Check fuel policy: full-to-full is most transparent.
  • Inspect vehicle for scratches or dents before signing.

Driving in Albania

Albania offers a diverse driving experience: coastal highways, mountain passes, and urban traffic. Know the rules and best practices to stay safe.

  • Road Conditions: Major routes are paved but rural roads can be uneven—drive slower on backcountry roads.
  • Signage: Most signs follow European norms but watch for local directional markers in Albanian.
  • Speed Limits: 50 km/h in cities, 80 km/h on open roads, 110 km/h on highways—speed cameras are common near towns.
  • Mountain Safety: Always carry water, blankets, and a physical map; network coverage can be spotty.

Discover Albania: History & Curiosities

Albania blends millennia of history with warm hospitality. From UNESCO World Heritage sites to modern urban hubs, there’s so much to explore.

Ancient Roots: Visit Butrint, an archaeological site founded by the Greeks in the 8th century BC, then expanded by Romans and Byzantines.

Cultural Gems: Experience a live iso-polyphonic concert in the Gjirokastër Old Town or browse Skanderbeg Square’s lively cafes in Tirana.

Local Curiosities: Albanians have a custom called Besa—an oath of honor—reflecting deep-rooted traditions of hospitality and trust.
